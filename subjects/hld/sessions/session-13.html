Session 13 · Case Study — IRCTC

Designing IRCTC: booking train tickets without ever selling the same seat twice

Today we put on our system-design hats and build something real: India's train-ticket booking system, IRCTC — one of the busiest websites on Earth. We'll lean on the ACID, consistency, and locking ideas from earlier sessions to solve it.

1 Requirements & the core challenge

Explain like I'm 5

Imagine one ice-cream truck with exactly 72 cones, and 50,000 kids sprinting toward it the moment it rings its bell. Everyone reaches in for a cone at the same second. The truck's whole job is simple to say but hard to do: give each cone to exactly one kid, and never hand the same cone to two kids. IRCTC is that truck — the cones are train seats, and the bell is "tatkal" tickets opening at 10 a.m.

Before we design anything, good engineers write down what the system must do. We split this into two buckets. Functional requirements are the features — what the user can do. Non-functional requirements are the qualities — how well the system must behave (fast? reliable? handles a crowd?).

Functional requirements (the features)

  • Search trains between a source and destination on a given date, with class (Sleeper, 3AC, etc.) and seat availability.
  • Check availability — how many seats are free in each class on each train.
  • Book a ticket — pick a train, select seats/berths, enter passenger details, and confirm.
  • Pay for the booking and get a confirmation (a PNR — the booking reference number).
  • Cancel a ticket and get a refund; the freed seat goes back into the pool.
  • Waitlist & RAC — when a class is sold out, users can join a waiting list and get promoted if others cancel.
  • Tatkal — a special last-minute quota that opens at a fixed time and sells out in seconds.

Non-functional requirements (the qualities)

QualityWhat it means hereTarget (illustrative)
ConsistencyThe seat count must be exactly right — a seat is never sold twice. This is non-negotiable.Strong consistency on the seat inventory.
AvailabilityThe site should stay up, especially during tatkal.99.9%+ uptime.
ScalabilityHandle a normal day and the tatkal stampede.~10–20× spike at 10 a.m.
LatencySearch and availability feel instant; booking confirms in a few seconds.Search < 300 ms; booking < few s.
DurabilityA confirmed booking is never lost, even if a server crashes.Survives crashes (ACID — Topic 2).

A back-of-the-envelope on the load

Let's get a feel for the numbers, because the design choices flow from them.

Worked example — sizing the tatkal spike

Suppose on a normal day IRCTC handles ~25 million users and books ~1 million tickets, spread across the day. That's a modest average of a few hundred bookings per second.

But tatkal opens at 10:00 a.m. sharp. In the first minute, tens of millions of requests can pile in. If 10 million people hit "search" and "book" in 60 seconds, that's roughly:

10,000,000 ÷ 60 ≈ 167,000 requests/second — a 100×+ burst over the quiet baseline, all aimed at a tiny pool of tatkal seats.

This is the defining shape of the problem: enormous, bursty read traffic, and a fierce fight over a small, shared, write-heavy resource (the seats).

The core challenge: double-booking under contention

Everything in this session orbits one problem. Two users, Asha and Ravi, both see "1 seat left" and both click "book" within the same millisecond. If our system naïvely does "read count → it's 1 → sell → write count = 0" for both of them, we sell the same seat twice. That's called a race condition (two operations racing, and the result depends on who wins). The fancier name for two requests reading-then-writing the same data without coordination is a lost update.

The one big idea

A train seat is a shared, finite resource that must be allocated exactly once. The entire art of building IRCTC is making thousands of simultaneous grabs for that seat behave as if they happened one at a time, in a clean order — without making everyone wait so long the site feels broken. ACID transactions and locking (Topic 2) are how we win this.

Why this is harder than a shopping cart

Amazon can oversell a phone and apologise later — they'll just ship it next week. IRCTC cannot put two strangers on the same berth; there is no "ship more seats." The inventory is hard-capped and the consequence of a mistake is a real human standing in a train with no place to sit. So we choose strong consistency for the seat inventory and accept a little extra latency for it.

Recap IRCTC must let users search, check availability, book, pay, cancel, waitlist, and use tatkal (functional), while being consistent, available, scalable, fast, and durable (non-functional). The killer problem is preventing double-booking of one seat under a massive, bursty stampede of concurrent users — a race condition we must tame with transactions and locks.

2 Avoiding double-booking with ACID & locking

Explain like I'm 5

Picture one bathroom and a line of people. The trick that stops two people barging in at once is the lock on the door. Whoever gets in first turns the lock; everyone else waits their turn. A database "lock" is exactly that: while one booking is touching a seat, it bolts the door so no one else can grab the same seat until it's done. The booking either fully completes (you walk out, job done) or fully un-happens (as if you never went in) — never half-way.

First, a refresher: ACID transactions

A transaction is a bundle of database steps that must all succeed or all fail together. Databases guarantee transactions are ACID — four properties we've met in earlier sessions, recapped here because they're the bedrock of our solution:

LetterPropertyWhat it gives the booking
AAtomicityReserve-seat + create-PNR + charge-payment either all happen or none do. No half-bookings.
CConsistencyRules are never violated — e.g. seats-booked can never exceed seats-total.
IIsolationConcurrent bookings don't trample each other; each behaves as if it ran alone. This is the one that beats double-booking.
DDurabilityOnce confirmed, the booking survives crashes and power cuts (written to disk/replicated).

Isolation levels — how strict is "don't trample"?

Isolation isn't all-or-nothing; databases offer isolation levels that trade safety for speed. From weakest to strongest:

LevelAllows the anomaly?Plain English
Read UncommittedDirty reads ✅You can see another transaction's half-finished work. Dangerous.
Read CommittedNon-repeatable reads ✅You only see committed data, but a re-read can change mid-transaction.
Repeatable ReadPhantom rows (sometimes) ✅Rows you've read won't change under you.
SerializableNothing — fully safeTransactions behave as if run one after another. Safest, slowest.

The lost update from Topic 1 (Asha and Ravi both reading "1 left") sneaks in at the weaker levels. To stop it cleanly, we either crank isolation up or, more commonly, take an explicit lock on the seat row. Locking gives us precise control without forcing the entire system to the slow Serializable level.

Pessimistic locking — "assume a fight, lock the door first"

Pessimistic locking assumes conflicts are likely (true during tatkal!), so it grabs a lock before touching the seat. In SQL this is SELECT ... FOR UPDATE: it reads the row and bolts it shut so no other transaction can read-for-update or modify it until you commit.

-- Pessimistic locking: bolt the seat row, then update it.
BEGIN;

-- Lock the row for this seat. Any other booking trying the same
-- row now WAITS here until we COMMIT or ROLLBACK.
SELECT status
FROM seats
WHERE train_id = 12951
  AND coach = 'B1'
  AND seat_no = 23
  AND journey_date = '2026-07-01'
FOR UPDATE;                       -- <-- the magic words

-- Now we are the ONLY one who can touch this seat.
-- App checks: is status = 'AVAILABLE'? If yes:
UPDATE seats
SET status = 'BOOKED', pnr = '8204417653'
WHERE train_id = 12951
  AND coach = 'B1'
  AND seat_no = 23
  AND journey_date = '2026-07-01';

COMMIT;                              -- door unlocks; next person proceeds
Worked example — Asha vs Ravi, with FOR UPDATE
  • t=0ms — Asha's transaction runs SELECT ... FOR UPDATE on seat B1-23. She gets the lock. Status reads AVAILABLE.
  • t=1ms — Ravi's transaction runs the same SELECT ... FOR UPDATE. The database makes him wait — the door is bolted.
  • t=8ms — Asha sets status to BOOKED and COMMITs. The lock releases.
  • t=8ms — Ravi's SELECT now returns, and he sees BOOKED. His app refuses the booking and offers him the waitlist.

The seat was sold exactly once. The lock turned a chaotic race into an orderly queue.

Optimistic locking — "assume no fight, but check before you commit"

Optimistic locking assumes conflicts are rare. It takes no lock up front. Instead each seat row carries a version number; you read the version, do your work, and at write-time say "update only if the version is still what I read." If someone slipped in first, the version changed, your update matches zero rows, and you retry.

-- Optimistic locking: no lock held; detect the clash at write time.

-- Step 1: read the seat AND its current version.
SELECT status, version
FROM seats
WHERE seat_id = 9912;
-- suppose this returns status='AVAILABLE', version=7

-- Step 2: try to book, but ONLY if version is still 7.
UPDATE seats
SET status  = 'BOOKED',
    pnr     = '8204417653',
    version = version + 1      -- bump it to 8
WHERE seat_id = 9912
  AND version  = 7           -- <-- the guard
  AND status   = 'AVAILABLE';

-- If 1 row updated  -> success.
-- If 0 rows updated -> someone else booked it first; retry or fail.
What just happened to Ravi here?

Both Asha and Ravi read version=7. Asha's UPDATE runs first, succeeds, and bumps version to 8. Ravi's UPDATE has WHERE version = 7 — but it's now 8, so 0 rows change. His app sees "0 rows" and knows he lost the race, without ever having locked anything.

Pessimistic vs optimistic — the trade-off

Pessimistic (FOR UPDATE)Optimistic (version check)
AssumesConflicts are commonConflicts are rare
When it locksUp front, holds it until commitNever holds a lock; checks at write
Cost of contentionOthers wait in line (can be slow, risks deadlock)Losers retry (wasted work if conflicts are frequent)
Best forHot rows with heavy contention — tatkal seatsLow-contention edits — e.g. updating a passenger's phone number
RiskDeadlocks & lock waits under loadRetry storms if everyone clashes
Watch out — the deadlock trap

Pessimistic locks can deadlock: transaction A locks seat 1 and waits for seat 2, while B locks seat 2 and waits for seat 1 — both stuck forever. Avoid it by always locking rows in a consistent order (e.g. ascending seat_id), keeping transactions short, and setting a lock-wait timeout so the database can abort and retry one of them.

Key takeaway

For the hot, fought-over seat inventory during tatkal, pessimistic SELECT ... FOR UPDATE is usually the right call — it serialises the fight cleanly. Use optimistic locking for the calmer parts of the system where clashes are rare and you'd rather not pay the cost of holding locks.

Recap ACID transactions (especially Isolation) are the foundation; isolation levels trade safety for speed. To stop double-booking, lock the seat. Pessimistic locking (FOR UPDATE) bolts the row first and is ideal for hot tatkal seats; optimistic locking uses a version check and retries on clash, ideal when conflicts are rare. Lock in a consistent order to dodge deadlocks.

3 Handling load spikes

Explain like I'm 5

Remember the 50,000 kids charging the ice-cream truck? You don't let all of them shove at the window at once — that breaks the truck. Instead you hand out numbered tickets and a calm helper calls "kids 1 to 50, please." Everyone else waits in a comfy waiting room. And once a kid says "I want the chocolate cone," you hold it for them for 2 minutes while they find their money — but if they dawdle, the cone goes back. That's the whole trick: smooth the stampede into a polite line, and hold things briefly so nobody pays twice or grabs twice.

From Topic 1 we know tatkal can be a 100×+ burst. A database doing SELECT ... FOR UPDATE can only serialise so fast before requests pile up and time out. The job here is to protect the precious booking core from the flood. We have five tools.

Tool 1 — A queue / virtual waiting room

Instead of letting every request slam the booking database, we put incoming users into a queue (a line) and admit them in controlled batches. Many big ticketing sites show a literal "you are number 14,233 in line" waiting room. This converts a spiky, unpredictable flood into a steady, survivable trickle the booking core can handle.

Tool 2 — Rate limiting

Rate limiting caps how many requests a single user (or IP) may send in a window. It blocks bots and scripts (a huge problem in tatkal) and stops one greedy client from hogging capacity. A classic implementation is the token-bucket algorithm: each user has a bucket that refills at a steady rate; each request spends a token; empty bucket = request rejected with HTTP 429 Too Many Requests.

Example — token bucket

Give each user a bucket of 5 tokens that refills 1 token/second. A human clicking normally never empties it. A bot firing 100 requests/second drains it instantly and gets 429ed after 5 — protecting everyone else.

Tool 3 — Seat-hold with a TTL

When a user picks a seat, we don't make them race through payment while holding a database lock — that would freeze the seat for everyone for minutes. Instead we create a short seat hold (a soft reservation) with a TTL (time-to-live, an auto-expiry). The seat is marked HELD for, say, 8 minutes while they pay. If they don't finish, the hold expires and the seat returns to the pool automatically.

-- Place a 8-minute hold, atomically, only if still available.
UPDATE seats
SET status      = 'HELD',
    held_by     = 'user_77421',
    hold_expiry = NOW() + INTERVAL '8 minutes'
WHERE seat_id = 9912
  AND status  = 'AVAILABLE';          -- only grab a free seat

-- A background job (or a check on read) frees expired holds:
UPDATE seats
SET status = 'AVAILABLE', held_by = NULL, hold_expiry = NULL
WHERE status = 'HELD'
  AND hold_expiry < NOW();           -- the TTL has passed

Seat holds are often kept in a fast in-memory store like Redis with a native TTL, so expiry is automatic and reads are blazing fast — the database remains the durable source of truth for confirmed bookings.

Tool 4 — Idempotent payment

During a spike, networks hiccup and users mash the "Pay" button. Without care, one person could be charged twice, or two bookings created for one payment. The fix is idempotency: an operation you can safely repeat and get the same single result. The client generates a unique idempotency key for the payment; the server records it, and any retry with the same key returns the original result instead of charging again.

-- The idempotency key makes retries safe.
-- A UNIQUE constraint guarantees one charge per key, even under a race.
INSERT INTO payments (idempotency_key, pnr, amount, status)
VALUES ('pay_a1b2c3d4', '8204417653', 1245.00, 'CHARGED')
ON CONFLICT (idempotency_key) DO NOTHING;   -- duplicate = no-op

-- If the row already existed, we DID NOT charge again;
-- we just return the existing payment's result to the user.

Tool 5 — Read replicas for browsing

Most traffic is reads: people searching trains and checking availability, not booking. Reads don't need the locked, write-heavy primary database. We spin up read replicas — copies of the database that serve read queries — and send all the search/browse traffic there. This frees the primary (the single authoritative write node) to focus purely on the precious booking writes.

Trade-off — replica lag

Replicas update a fraction of a second behind the primary (replication lag), so a search might briefly show "2 seats left" when 1 was just booked. That's fine for browsing — it's only a hint. The final, authoritative seat check happens against the primary inside the locked booking transaction (Topic 2), where correctness is guaranteed. Eventually-consistent reads, strongly-consistent writes.

Worked example — the five tools in one tatkal flow
  1. 10:00:00 — 167k req/s arrive. The waiting room holds most; admits ~2k/s.
  2. A bot tries 500 req/s and is rate-limited to a trickle of 429s.
  3. Admitted users browse availability on read replicas — fast, no load on the primary.
  4. A user picks seat B1-23; we place an 8-minute seat hold in Redis.
  5. They pay; the idempotency key ensures their double-click charges them once.
  6. On success, the booking core confirms the seat against the primary inside a locked transaction. Done.
Recap To survive spikes: a queue/waiting room smooths the flood into a steady trickle; rate limiting blocks bots and hogs; seat holds with a TTL let users pay without freezing seats forever; idempotent payment makes retries safe; and read replicas absorb all the browsing so the primary database is reserved for the consistent, locked booking writes.

4 Multi-service architecture

Explain like I'm 5

Think of a restaurant. One person doesn't take your order, cook, handle the cash, and phone you when food's ready — there's a waiter, a chef, a cashier, and a host. Each is great at one job, and they hand work to each other. We build IRCTC the same way: separate services, each owning one job, talking to each other. If the "phone you" person is out sick, you still get your food — the others keep working. That's why we split things up.

A single giant program (a monolith) that does everything is hard to scale and fragile — one bug or one overloaded feature can sink the whole site. So we split IRCTC into independent microservices, each owning its own data and scaling on its own.

The services

ServiceJobNotes on scaling/consistency
Search / AvailabilityFind trains, show seat counts.Read-heavy. Scales wide on read replicas + cache. Eventual consistency is fine.
BookingHold seats, allocate them, create the PNR.The consistency-critical core. ACID + locking (Topic 2). The primary database lives here.
PaymentCharge the card, talk to banks/UPI.Must be idempotent (Topic 3). Slow & external — never block a lock on it.
NotificationSend SMS/email/app confirmation with the PNR.Best-effort, asynchronous. A failure here must not fail the booking.

The consistency problem between services

Here's the catch. In Topic 2, atomicity was easy: one database, one transaction, all-or-nothing. But now booking, payment, and notification live in different services with different databases. There's no single transaction spanning all of them. So what if we reserve the seat, but the payment fails? We'd have a held seat and no money — a mess.

The big idea — sagas & eventual consistency

When you can't have one big ACID transaction across services, you use a saga: a sequence of local transactions, one per service, where each step has a matching compensating action that undoes it if a later step fails. The system isn't consistent at every instant, but it becomes correct shortly after — eventual consistency. (Recall replica lag from Topic 3 — same family of idea.)

Worked example — a saga that fails and self-heals
  1. Booking service holds seat B1-23 (local transaction ✅).
  2. Payment service tries to charge ₹1,245 — the bank declines ❌.
  3. The saga triggers the compensating action: Booking service releases the hold, seat returns to AVAILABLE.
  4. Notification service tells the user "payment failed, please retry."

No money taken, no seat stuck, no double-booking. The system briefly held a seat it shouldn't have, then healed itself — eventual consistency in action.

How services talk: events & the outbox

Services coordinate by passing events through a message queue (like Kafka or RabbitMQ) rather than calling each other directly and waiting. The Booking service emits a SeatReserved event; Payment listens and acts; on success it emits PaymentConfirmed; Notification listens and sends the SMS. This asynchronous style means a slow or down Notification service never blocks a booking.

The outbox pattern (so events aren't lost)

A subtle danger: what if Booking commits the seat but crashes before publishing the event? The transactional outbox fixes this: the service writes the event into an outbox table in the same local transaction as the seat change. A separate process then reliably ships rows from the outbox to the queue. Either both the seat change and the event survive, or neither does — preserving atomicity within the service.

If any step before the commit fails, the saga's compensations unwind everything cleanly.

Watch out — don't make payment part of the lock

A classic beginner mistake is holding the seat's database FOR UPDATE lock while calling the payment provider. Payment can take seconds and may time out — you'd freeze that hot seat for everyone the whole time. Always: hold (soft) → pay (no DB lock) → then take the short lock only to flip the seat to BOOKED.

Recap Split IRCTC into search/availability, booking, payment, and notification services, each owning its data and scaling independently. Because there's no single transaction across them, use a saga of local transactions with compensating actions for eventual consistency, coordinated by asynchronous events (with the outbox pattern so events aren't lost). The end-to-end flow is search → hold → pay → confirm (locked) → notify (async).

5 Typeahead approach 1 — Tries

Explain like I'm 5

This session's case study has a second half: the search box that finishes your sentence. You type "What" and it instantly suggests the most popular things people search that start with "What". Imagine a giant tree of letters: start at the root, walk down one letter at a time (W → h → a → t), and everything hanging below the spot you land on is a possible ending. Show the 5 most-searched endings. That tree-of-letters is a trie.

The feature is called typeahead (or autocomplete). When the user types the partial query "What", we must show the top 5 search queries that are a strict prefix match. The classic data-structures answer is a trie (prefix tree).

What each trie node stores

  • children — links to all the subsequent letters.
  • isTerminal — does a complete search query end at this node?
  • count — only meaningful if isTerminal: the number of times this exact query has been searched.
class TrieNode {
    TrieNode children[63]     // a-z + A-Z + 0-9 + space
    long count;               // how many times has this
                              // query been searched
}

Query: typeahead(partialQuery)

→
📍

Once we reach the node matching the partialQuery, all possible suggestions live in the subtree of that node. We'd have to walk the entire subtree (every query that matches the prefix) to find the top 5.

Watch out — the subtree can be enormous

Walking the whole subtree is highly time-consuming. There are potentially billions of entries under a short prefix like "wh". Scanning that on every keystroke is a non-starter.

The fix: data-augmentation

We can speed this up with data-augmentation: at every node, pre-compute and store the top 5 results. Now typeahead just reads the answer off the node — no subtree scan. The price is that updates become (slightly) slower.

Query: log_search(searchQuery) (update the count)

  • Traverse to the node that matches this searchQuery.
  • Increment its count.
  • If augmented, also update the stored top-5 data for all nodes from here up to the root.

Sharding a trie

Q: What will be the sharding key?

Sharding keyCardinalityProblem
First letter — every query starting with a goes to the same shardLow (only 26 possible children)Uneven distribution: far more queries start with a than z.
First 3 letters — every query starting with abc goes to the same shardHigh: 26³ ≈ 17,000Still uneven: many more queries start with why than zxz.

Note: putting all "a" queries on the same shard does not mean a gets a dedicated shard — a single shard may hold several starting letters. We only guarantee that all queries with the same prefix land together.

To fix the load imbalance with the 3-letter key, we manually group the starting triples so rare ones are clubbed together:

  • Queries starting with why are very frequent ⇒ give them a dedicated shard.
  • Queries starting with zxz are rare ⇒ group them with other rare prefixes (zyx + zxy + zxz + …).
Q: Which existing databases support tries?

There's no popular database built for storing tries. Tries exist only as a 3rd-party extension in some databases, but none was built specifically for them. So with this approach you'd have to build your own DB — which is exactly what pushes us toward approach 2.

Recap A trie answers typeahead(prefix) by walking the prefix and scanning the subtree for the top 5 — but the subtree can hold billions of entries, so we data-augment every node with its pre-computed top 5 (fast reads, slightly slower writes that must propagate to the root). Shard by first-N letters, then manually group rare prefixes to even out load. No off-the-shelf DB stores tries, so you'd build your own.

6 Typeahead approach 2 — Hashmap / Key-Value

Explain like I'm 5

Forget the fancy tree. What if we just kept a giant cheat-sheet? On the left column, every possible thing someone might have typed so far ("wha", "what", "what d"). On the right column, the 3 best suggestions for that. When you type, we just look up your row and read the answer. That cheat-sheet is a hashmap (key → value), and looking something up is basically instant.

The big idea — augmentation IS a cache

As a DSA person, we'd build a trie and augment each node with its top-5. As an HLD person, we realise we're effectively just caching the top-5 results for every possible prefix. So: prefix == TrieNode and data-augmentation == cache. We can drop the trie entirely and store this as a plain key-value cache.

Two databases

  • Search Frequency DB — for each actual searchQuery, stores its count.
  • Top Suggestions DB (cache) — for each possible prefix we'd show suggestions for, stores the top-k suggestions.

Query: typeahead(partial_query)

If someone types "what i", we just go to the suggestions DB and look up that key. This is very fast: hashmaps have O(1) lookup. (Technically it's O(l) in the key length l — but keys in a hashmap should never be long; if your hashmap keys are long, you're using hashmaps wrong.) Here the average key length was assumed to be 10 chars and the max 50 chars. No computation needed — just fetch the value for the key.

Worked example — sizing the cache with Redis

Our non-functional needs: ultra-low latency (< 10 ms) and high read throughput (10 million reads/second at peak).

  • A Redis lookup has latency ≤ 1 ms. ✅
  • A single Redis server easily handles 100,000+ reads/writes per second.
  • So we need only ~100 Redis servers (Google had over 10 million servers as of 2020 — trivial).

Query: log_search(search_query)

When someone searches "what does the fox say":

  1. Update the count in the frequency DB — dead simple: redis.inc(searchQuery).
  2. Update the cache (top suggestions DB). Which entries? Updating "what does the fox say" can't change the suggestions for "how" — only the prefixes of the search query itself are affected: wha, what, what d, …, what does the fox say.
Worked example — a count flip re-orders suggestions

Suppose "what does a fox eat?" climbs from 1900 to 2100. It now outranks "what does the fox say" (2000), so the affected prefixes are re-sorted:

what d
[
  what does a fox eat?         =>  2100
  what does the fox say        =>  2000
]

what do
[
  what does a fox eat?         =>  2100
  what does the fox say        =>  2000
]

The "wha" / "what" lists also pick up the new entry.

Watch out — this makes the system read AND write heavy

The average query is ~10 letters, so ~10 prefixes need updating per log_search. That's 1 + 10 = 11 writes per search. At 1 million log_searches/second that's 1,000,000 × 11 = 11 million writes/second — on top of the 10 million reads/second. No database in the world is optimized for both heavy reads AND heavy writes. We'll fix this in the next two topics.

Sharding & databases for this approach

Sharding is now automatic — based on hash(key). And unlike tries, there's a whole category of databases with first-class hashmap support: key-value databases are basically hashmaps distributed across servers — Redis / Memcached / DynamoDB / …

Recap Augmenting a trie is really just caching top-k per prefix, so store it as a key-value system: a Frequency DB (query → count) plus a Top Suggestions cache (prefix → top-k). Reads are O(1) and Redis-fast (~100 servers for 10M reads/s). But each log_search touches ~11 keys, making the system both read- and write-heavy — a problem no single DB solves, motivating the optimizations next.

7 Optimizing reads & writes

Explain like I'm 5

A library that lots of people read from is easy — keep copies everywhere. A library where lots of people are constantly scribbling new notes is harder. A library where people both read tons AND scribble tons at the same time is a nightmare. The trick is to figure out which kind you have, and lean on a fast scratchpad (a cache) for the easy direction so the slow, careful book (the database) only does what it must.

Generalising beyond typeahead: how you optimize depends on whether the system is read-heavy, write-heavy, or both — and on whether you can tolerate eventual consistency.

If the system is read-heavy (not write-heavy)

  • If eventual consistency is OK ⇒ absorb the reads in the cache, and optimize the DB for writes.
  • If immediate consistency is needed ⇒ either:
    • incur extra write latency with a write-through cache (DB optimized for writes), or
    • optimize the DB for reads — writes get slower (fine, there are few), and both reads & writes go to the DB. Slower than the cached approach.

If the system is write-heavy (not read-heavy)

  • Regardless of eventual vs immediate consistency ⇒ optimize the DB for writes.
  • If reads are significant, absorb them in the cache; if not, let them hit the DB and be slow.
  • Your cache cannot handle the writes — a write-back cache would lose data if the cache server crashes with unsynced changes.

If the system is both read AND write heavy

This is very VERY difficult (exactly our typeahead situation). Two escape routes:

  • Reduce the writes:
    • Batching — forces eventual consistency: wait for a bunch of requests to collect, then process them in one go.
    • Sampling — forces data loss: don't process all requests, only some.
  • If you can't reduce writes, shard more. Sharding improves both reads & writes — but then you can't join across shards efficiently, so any query needing data from multiple shards is slow.
Key takeaway

For typeahead we can afford eventual consistency and even some data loss (it's a non-functional requirement — suggestions only need to be relevant, not perfectly ranked). That permission is what unlocks batching and sampling to crush the write load.

Recap Read-heavy ⇒ absorb reads in a cache (or optimize DB for reads if you need immediate consistency). Write-heavy ⇒ optimize the DB for writes; caches can't safely absorb writes. Both ⇒ very hard: either reduce writes (batching = eventual consistency, sampling = data loss) or shard more (helps reads & writes, but kills cross-shard joins).

8 Reducing writes — batching & sampling

Explain like I'm 5

If 10 guests want tea, you don't boil one tiny cup, run it over, come back, boil the next — you make one big pot and serve everyone at once. That's batching. And if you want to know who a whole country voted for, you don't ask all 10 crore people — you ask a small random handful outside polling booths and trust the trend. That's sampling. Both let us do far less work while still getting an answer that's good enough.

Batching / batch processing

Instead of doing each task one by one, wait for a lot of tasks to pile up, then do them all in one go. For typeahead: on each log_search, instead of updating the suggestions DB immediately, we wait for the count to increase by a fixed batch size (say 1000) before pushing the update.

Earlier (no batching)

fn log_search(search_query):         // 1 million qps
    frequency_db.inc(search_query)      // 1 million writes/s
    update_prefixes(search_query)

fn update_prefixes(search_query):
    for i = 3 ... len(search_query) - 1  // 10 iterations
        prefix = search_query[:i]
        ... logic to update the suggestions list
            in the suggestions db
        // 10 million writes/s

With batching

fn log_search(search_query):         // 1 million qps
    updated_count = frequency_db.inc(search_query)
    // 1 million writes/s

    if updated_count % 1000 == 0:
        // update prefixes only when the count has
        // changed by the batch size
        update_prefixes(search_query)

fn update_prefixes(search_query):
    ...   // (1 million / 1000 qps) * 10 updates
          // 10,000 writes/sec
Worked example — the write count collapses

Earlier: 1M writes/s (freq DB) + 10M writes/s (suggestions DB) = 11M writes/s.

After batching: 1M writes/s (freq DB) + (10M / 1000) = 1.01M writes/s.

We can't go below 1M/s — that's how often we at least update the counts in the frequency DB.

What batching costs you

Batching does not cause data loss — the frequency data is always up-to-date. It only causes delays (stale reads) in the suggestions updates.

Sampling

Analogy — election exit polls
  • A country-wide election has 1 crore voters; the actual count takes several days.
  • News channels want to predict the winner with high accuracy before results are out.
  • They can't talk to all 1 crore voters. Instead they stand outside a few polling booths and ask a few random exiting voters "who did you vote for?".
  • Using just that small sample they estimate the winner. If the channel is unbiased, the exit-poll result almost perfectly matches the real result.

If you draw an unbiased sample from a population, any trend that holds in the population also holds in the sample. A good way to get unbiased samples is to sample uniformly at random.

Earlier (every search logged)

// search service
fn search(query):
    ...
    make an API call to typeahead service's log_search endpoint

// typeahead service
fn log_search(search_query):
    frequency_db.inc(search_query)
    update_prefixes(search_query)

fn update_prefixes(search_query):
    for i = 3 ... len(search_query) - 1
        prefix = search_query[:i]
        ... logic to update the suggestions list
            in the suggestions db

With sampling

// search service
fn search(query):
    ...
    if rand() < 0.001:
        // with probability 0.1%, make the following call
        make an API call to typeahead service's log_search endpoint

// typeahead service
    ...

For 99.9% of searches we don't even update the counts (just ignore them). For a random 0.1%, we call log_search and update both counts and suggestions. Instead of 1M + 10M writes/s, we're back to (1M + 10M) / 1000 = 10,000 writes/second.

Won't the counts be inaccurate under sampling?

Yes — each count is effectively divided by ~1000 (not exactly, since it's random). But it doesn't matter: the suggestions shown to users stay highly relevant.

Won't the suggestions be slightly out of order?

Yes. And again it doesn't matter — relevance is preserved, which is all the feature needs.

With sampling, won't infrequent queries get tiny counts or be missed entirely?

Yes, that can happen — and it's a feature, not a bug! A rare query would never be a typeahead suggestion anyway (it isn't popular). Sampling automatically prunes the majority (99.9%) of "bad" queries.

What sampling costs you

Sampling can lose individual data points, but it won't lose the overall trends. Use it only when data loss is acceptable.

Recap Batching updates the suggestions DB only every Nth count (e.g. 1000), cutting 11M writes/s to ~1.01M writes/s — costing stale reads but never data loss. Sampling only logs a random 0.1% of searches, cutting writes to ~10k/s — costing data loss of individual points but preserving trends, and conveniently discarding rare queries that would never be suggested anyway.

9 Future scope — recency, geo, typos

Explain like I'm 5

A good autocomplete doesn't just know what's popular forever — it knows what's hot this week (recency), what people near you care about (location), what you personally search, and it forgives your typos. These extras turn a decent suggestion box into a great one.

Recency factor

When a big event happens (COVID lockdown declared, election results out, a high-profile arrest, Federer wins Wimbledon), the query is trending. Even if "why is the sky blue?" has a higher all-time count, a trending "what happened in Nepal?" (recent + popular) should rank higher when someone types "wh".

Approach 1 — separate counts per window

For each query keep three counts: Total, Last Week, Last Day.

total:why is the sky blue?    => 5000
 week:why is the sky blue?    =>  200
  day:why is the sky blue?    =>   50
total:what happened in Nepal? =>  500
 week:what happened in Nepal? =>  300
  day:what happened in Nepal? =>  100

To find top-k: find top-k from total, from weekly, and from daily, then merge using a scoring function. Goal: give more weight to the recent counts.

Better solution — decay the historical counts

Simply decay every query's count by a fixed factor each period — e.g. after each day, decrease the total count by 10% (multiply by 0.9) before adding the day's new count.

Worked example — a steady query converges

"who has control over nukes?" gets a steady 1000/day: [1000, 1000, …] (raw total would be 100,000).

day 1: 1000
day 2: 90% of 1000 + 1000  => 1900
day 3: 90% of 1900 + 1000  => 2710
day 4: 90% of 2710 + 1000  => 3439
...
day 100: 1000 * (1 + 0.9 + 0.9^2 + 0.9^3 + ...)
       = 1000 * 1/(1 - 0.9)
       = 10,000
Worked example — a fresh burst overtakes it

"who won the wimbledon" is silent then bursts: [0, 0, …, 5000, 5000, 5000] (raw total just 15,000).

day 97: 0
day 98: 5000
day 99: 5000 * 0.9 + 5000  =>  9,500
day 100: 9500 * 0.9 + 5000 => 13,550

Its decayed score (13,550) now beats the steady query's (10,000) — recency wins. If a decayed count drops below a threshold (say 0), remove the entry.

Geolocation-based personalization

  • From the user's request, find their location via IP address.
  • Build a separate database per location ⇒ shard the DB by country_id. An Indian user's request only hits the Indian shard.
"global: what does the fox say" => 1000
"India:  what does the fox say" =>   10
"USA:    what does the fox say" =>   10

When an Indian user types "wha", find suggestions for the "wha" prefix and the "India:wha" prefix, merge them, and return the final set.

User-based personalization

  • Do this purely on the client side: the browser stores the user's search/browsing history.
  • The browser builds typeahead suggestions from the user's local data, and fires a backend request for the global suggestions, then merges the two lists.
  • Google actually pulls this from the backend too — because it maintains your search & browsing history in its backend DB.

Handling typos

  • On each search, besides updating the count of search_query, also update the count of spell_corrected(search_query).
  • When a user types something, find suggestions for partial_query and include the suggestions for spell_corrected(partial_query).
def spell_corrected(input: str) -> str:
    """Corrects the spellings of the words within the
       input string, and returns the corrected string.
       Note:
           - each word in the input is spell corrected
           - gracefully handles partial words
    """

You can keep a dictionary and compute the edit-distance between the user's words and the dictionary words. Peter Norvig's classic essay "How to Write a Spelling Corrector" is the canonical reference.

Recap Make typeahead smarter with: recency (decay counts ~10%/day so trending queries overtake stale popular ones), geolocation (shard by country, merge global + local prefixes), user personalization (merge client-side history with backend suggestions), and typo handling (also track/serve spell_corrected() via edit-distance against a dictionary).

★ Putting it all together

You just designed one of the hardest consistency problems in the real world. Here's the one-paragraph story that connects all four topics:

IRCTC must let millions search, book, pay, and cancel train tickets while never selling the same seat twice — a brutal race condition that explodes during the tatkal spike. We beat the double-booking with ACID transactions and locking: pessimistic SELECT ... FOR UPDATE for the hot seats, optimistic version checks for the calm parts. We survive the spike with a waiting-room queue, rate limiting, short-lived seat holds with a TTL, idempotent payment, and read replicas for all the browsing. And we keep it maintainable by splitting into microservices — search, booking, payment, notification — coordinated by a saga with compensating actions and asynchronous events, giving strong consistency where it matters (the seat) and eventual consistency everywhere else.

Quick self-check

What is the single hardest problem in designing IRCTC, and why?

Preventing double-booking of the same seat under massive concurrent demand. The seat is a hard-capped resource that must be allocated exactly once — unlike a shop, you can't oversell and apologise. The tatkal burst makes thousands of users fight for the same seat in the same instant.

You're booking the last tatkal seat — heavy contention. Pessimistic or optimistic locking?

Pessimistic (SELECT ... FOR UPDATE). When conflicts are common, locking the row up front serialises the fight cleanly. Optimistic locking would cause a storm of failed retries when everyone clashes on the same hot row.

Why use read replicas for the search/availability screens?

Most traffic is reads, and reads don't need the locked write path. Replicas absorb the browsing load so the primary is free for consistent booking writes. The small replica lag is acceptable because the authoritative seat check happens against the primary inside the locked transaction.

What is a seat-hold TTL and what problem does it solve?

A short soft reservation (e.g. 8 minutes) that auto-expires. It lets a user pay without holding a database lock the whole time, and if they abandon checkout, the seat returns to the pool automatically instead of being stuck forever.

Why can't we use one ACID transaction across booking, payment, and notification?

They are separate services with separate databases, so no single transaction spans them. Instead we use a saga: a sequence of local transactions, each with a compensating action to undo it if a later step fails — giving eventual consistency.

Why must payment never run while holding the seat's FOR UPDATE lock?

Payment is slow and external (seconds, may time out). Holding the row lock during it would freeze that hot seat for every other user. Correct order: soft-hold the seat, pay with no DB lock, then take a brief lock only to flip the seat to BOOKED.
